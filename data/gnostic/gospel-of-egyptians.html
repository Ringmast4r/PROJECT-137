The Gospel of the Egyptians

An early Christian text surviving only in fragments

About This Text

The Gospel of the Egyptians was an early Christian gospel likely written in the 2nd century, probably in Egypt. It is known only from a handful of quotations by Clement of Alexandria and other church fathers. The full text has been lost.

This gospel should not be confused with the Coptic "Holy Book of the Great Invisible Spirit" (also called "Gospel of the Egyptians") found in the Nag Hammadi library, which is a completely different Sethian Gnostic text.

Surviving Fragments

Fragment 1 - Dialogue with Salome

Source: Clement of Alexandria, Stromateis 3.9.63-64

When Salome asked, "How long will death have power?" the Lord answered: "As long as you women bear children."

Salome said: "Then I have done well in not bearing children."

The Lord answered: "Eat every plant, but do not eat that which has bitterness."

Fragment 2 - On Male and Female

Source: Clement of Alexandria, Stromateis 3.13.92

When Salome asked when what she had inquired about would be known, the Lord said: "When you have trampled on the garment of shame and when the two become one and the male with the female is neither male nor female."

Fragment 3 - The Kingdom

Source: Clement of Alexandria, Stromateis 3.9.64

"I have come to destroy the works of the female."

Note: Clement interprets "female" as referring to desire/passion, not women.

Fragment 4 - Another Version

Source: 2 Clement 12:2 (possibly quoting this gospel)

"When the Lord himself was asked by someone when his kingdom would come, he said: 'When the two shall be one, and the outside as the inside, and the male with the female neither male nor female.'"

Distinctive Features

  • Encratite Themes: The gospel appears to promote sexual continence and celibacy
  • Salome as Interlocutor: The woman Salome is the primary questioner
  • Unity of Opposites: Teaching that male and female must become one
  • Anti-Procreation: Childbearing is associated with death's power

Theological Interpretation

Clement of Alexandria quotes this gospel but interprets it spiritually rather than literally:

  • "The female" represents passion and desire, not women
  • "Male and female becoming one" refers to the soul rising above passion
  • The teaching is about spiritual purity, not physical abstinence

However, the Encratites (a celibate Christian sect) interpreted these sayings literally to support their rejection of marriage and procreation.

Historical Context

Church fathers who mention this gospel:

  • Clement of Alexandria (c. 150-215) - Primary source of quotations
  • Hippolytus (c. 170-235) - Mentions Encratites used it
  • Epiphanius (c. 310-403) - Notes it was used by various groups

Relationship to Other Texts

The themes in the Gospel of the Egyptians have parallels in:

  • Gospel of Thomas (saying 22 on male/female becoming one)
  • Gospel of Philip (bridal chamber theology)
  • 2 Clement (early Christian writing)

These parallels suggest a common tradition about the overcoming of gender division in salvation.
